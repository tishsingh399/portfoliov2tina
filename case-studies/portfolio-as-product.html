What this proves

The constraints were the point.

  • Making good decisions without the right tools
  • Balancing design and implementation across a full product
  • Shipping within real constraints, not ideal ones
  • Thinking beyond visuals — structure, judgment, and trade-offs
  • Owning the outcome end-to-end, including security and deployment
Key Decisions

Seven decisions that shaped the outcome.

01
Treat the portfolio like a product, not a gallery

Most portfolios are collections. I designed this as a guided experience — the homepage leads directly to key work, each case study follows a consistent structure, and the goal is to reduce thinking for the person reviewing it.

02
Keep visuals restrained, add personality intentionally

I introduced one 8-bit motif as a recurring element. It acts as a signature — one accent per page, not background overload. Personality without noise.

03
Prioritize clarity over technical complexity

Instead of complex frameworks, I used simple HTML and CSS. The system stays understandable and maintainable. Less surface area for things to break. Faster to load.

04
Design for real usage, not just presentation

Recruiters scan quickly. Key information is visible within seconds. Case studies are easy to enter, skim, and exit. Navigation stays predictable throughout.

05
Build within constraints instead of waiting for perfect skills

I did not wait until I "learned frontend properly." I worked within what I knew, made trade-offs, and focused on delivering a functional, complete product. Shipping beats perfecting.

06
Use AI as a design collaborator, not a replacement

I used Antigravity and Claude Code — AI coding agents — not to design for me, but to extend my ability to ship. I directed the fixes: identified the bugs, decided what to change, and validated the results. The AI executed targeted CSS changes, ran accessibility audits on color ratios, and deployed to Vercel via CLI. The decisions were mine. The velocity was collaborative.

07
Write a machine-readable contract when AI keeps breaking your rules

When the AI repeatedly stretched images to full width and ignored sizing rules I'd stated in conversation, I wrote DESIGN.md — a constraint document checked into the repo. It defines image classes, forbids object-fit cropping, sets max-widths, and specifies when to use .cs-img vs .cs-img-full. The file exists not for humans to read first — it's for the AI. It worked.

The Problem

Most portfolio tools optimize for speed or polish, not control.

Templates made everything feel the same. Heavily visual builders leaned toward animation and presentation over clarity. Fully custom builds required deeper engineering knowledge than I had.

I needed something in between:

  • Control over structure and interaction
  • Ability to reflect my thinking, not just visuals
  • Simple enough to build and maintain myself
  • Fast and reliable when deployed
"I treated the portfolio itself as a product. Not just something to display work — something that reflects how I think, make decisions, and handle constraints."
My Role

End-to-end, on purpose.

I handled everything: defining structure and content, designing layout and interaction, writing all case studies, building the site using HTML and CSS, and handling deployment and basic security considerations.

This was not about writing perfect code. It was about making correct decisions within my skill set.

Security + Deployment

Intentional, even at small scale.

I deployed using Vercel — reliable hosting, fast global CDN, and automatic HTTPS out of the box. Even for a portfolio, basic security and stability matter. These were not afterthoughts.

■
Static site — no backend, no database, nothing to exploit server-side. Minimal attack surface by design.
■
No unnecessary third-party scripts — Google Fonts is the only external dependency. No analytics trackers, no third-party widgets, no hidden surface area.
■
Clean asset references — all links and assets are controlled. No user-generated content, no dynamic URL construction, no injection vectors.
■
Automatic HTTPS via Vercel — TLS enforced, certificate auto-renewed, no manual certificate management.
■
Tested before deployment — load behavior, mobile rendering, and link integrity checked manually. No surprises in production.

This was not enterprise-level security. It was intentional and appropriate for the scope.

AI-Assisted Debugging & Deployment

Antigravity as a force multiplier.

After the initial build, I used Antigravity — Google DeepMind's agentic AI coding assistant — to debug and ship the next iteration. This wasn't about handing off design decisions. It was about extending my ability to execute with precision.

Here's exactly what was fixed in a single session:

01
Uneven card widths — the work grid used grid-template-columns: 2fr 1fr 1fr, making the first case study card twice as wide. Fixed to repeat(3, 1fr) for equal columns.
02
Dark mode contrast overhaul — backgrounds, text, muted tones, and borders were all too close in luminance. Rewrote the entire :root color palette with WCAG AA-passing contrast ratios and added a teal accent for visual variety.
03
Vercel deployment via CLI — connected the repo to GitHub (tishsingh399/portfoliov2tina), pushed fixes, and deployed to production using vercel --prod. Live in under 10 seconds.
"I directed the diagnosis. AI executed the surgery. The decisions were mine — the velocity was collaborative."
Iteration 2: Claude Code

4 hours straight. Every bug had a story. Most got solved.

After the initial build with Antigravity, I spent 4+ consecutive hours iterating using Claude Code — Anthropic's AI coding agent inside VS Code. This pass was real: real bugs, real constraints, and the constant tension of working with an AI that had its own ideas about what "correct" meant.

01
Replaced abstract pip-stat cards — The old hero cards showed pixel bar charts (HIERARCHY: ████░) that looked interesting but told a recruiter nothing. I asked for them to be replaced 4 times before they fully disappeared. Eventually forced the change by specifying the exact CSS class to delete.
02
Card sequence ignored repeatedly — I had a specific order: Design System → Inline Alert → Platform Nav → Login. The AI kept reverting or reordering. I had to screenshot the sequence and paste it explicitly before it held.
03
All images were AI-generated — Discovered mid-session that every image the AI had added to my case studies were AI-generated stock fakes: "NEXUS CORE" sci-fi dashboards, "AETHER SYSTEMS" mockups, "QuantumFlow" neon diagrams. None of it was real work. Removed all of them.
04
Interactive resume and PDF were 404ing — Both files existed locally but were never committed to git. Vercel only deploys what's in the repo. Fixed by explicitly staging and pushing each file.
05
Theme toggle missing on 3 detailed pages — The detailed case study pages (Shield, Inline Alert, Platform Nav) were dark-mode only with no toggle. Added the CSS, button, and localStorage script to each.
06
The image problem — then solved — Mid-session discovery: every "screenshot" Claude had added was AI-generated fiction. NEXUS CORE dashboards. AETHER SYSTEMS mockups. QuantumFlow neon diagrams. None of it was real work. I tracked down the actual screenshots across three Desktop folders, renamed them with intent (shield-component-library.png, login-final-option9.png), deleted all 12 fakes, and dropped in 20+ real images.
07
Images at 100% width — a design contract — After solving the fake image problem, a new one surfaced: every image stretched to fill the full container. Some were 3000px wide screenshots rendered at viewport width. I wrote a DESIGN.md file — a machine-readable contract defining exactly how images should be sized, what CSS classes to use (.cs-img, .cs-img-full), and explicit rules against object-fit cropping or 100% width overrides. The AI followed it from that point forward.
"Each iteration isn't about adding features. It's about removing friction between the work and the person reading it."
The Domain

tinasingh.app — a real commitment.

I bought a custom domain. That's not a technical decision — it's a product decision. A custom domain signals that this portfolio isn't a side experiment or a Notion doc shared as a link. It's something I own, maintain, and stand behind. The .app TLD enforces HTTPS by spec, which also meant I couldn't accidentally ship an insecure site even if I tried.

Connecting the domain to Vercel took under 10 minutes. The DNS propagation took longer than the actual work. But the decision to buy it — to treat this portfolio as a live product with a real address — was a more significant step than the technical setup.

Constraints

Working with what you have.

The biggest constraint was my own technical depth. I had to balance what I wanted to build with what I could realistically execute without turning this into a long-term engineering project.

There was also a constant risk of over-designing instead of finishing. I had to stay focused on shipping a complete version first, then iterating.

Outcome

What changed.

Before
Template-constrained or engineer-dependent. No clear ownership of the final output.
After
Fully owned, customizable portfolio. A structure that reflects how I think, not just what I've made.
Before
Waiting for the right tool, the right time, the right skill level.
After
A working example of bridging design and implementation. Something I can iterate on over time.
Why This Mattered

More than a portfolio.

This project shows how I approach ambiguous problems, work within constraints, and make decisions across design and implementation.

It reflects a shift in how I work. Instead of relying on tools to define the outcome, I define the structure and use tools to execute it.

The Antigravity and Claude Code iterations prove something else: I can work with AI tools effectively — not as a crutch, but as a deliberate extension of my process. When the AI drifted (fake images, wrong widths, ignored instructions), I didn't debug it with complaints — I debugged it with a design contract. DESIGN.md is the artifact of that. The decisions were mine. The discipline was mine.

What I Learned
"You don't need to be a frontend engineer to build something functional and thoughtful. You need to understand what matters, make clear decisions, and accept trade-offs."

Constraint-driven work often produces better outcomes than waiting for ideal conditions. The discipline of building with limits forces you to be precise about what actually matters.
